the model

surveillance isn't a side effect of modern technology. it's the business model.

the product was never the app. the product was never the service. the product is the map of your behavior, built from ten thousand small observations, sold to people you've never heard of, used for purposes you were never told.

  • every search query is a window into intent.
  • location data reveals your doctor, your lawyer, your place of worship — without a warrant.
  • purchase history predicts political affiliation with ~87% accuracy.
  • voice assistants process audio in local buffers. the "wake word" is the threshold. not the start.
  • smart TVs report viewing habits. the TV watching you watch.

state-level

corporations sell. governments acquire. the distinction matters less than you think, because the data flows between both.

  • PRISM, XKEYSCORE, MUSCULAR — ████████████ — these are the ones with names.
  • stingrays (IMSI catchers) impersonate cell towers. your phone connects. they log it. no warrant needed in most jurisdictions.
  • license plate readers scan an average of 1,800 plates per hour per unit. your commute is a database entry.
  • fusion centers share intelligence laterally — between agencies, between states, sometimes between countries.
  • predictive policing algorithms flag people for things they haven't done.
every light is a node. every node is a record.
why, actually

the cynical answer: money and control. the accurate answer: the same.

attention is a resource. behavior is predictable when you have enough data. predictable behavior can be nudged. nudged behavior is monetizable. monetizable behavior funds more data collection.

it's not malevolent in the way a villain is malevolent. it's systemic. structural. which is worse, because there's no one to blame and nowhere to direct the anger.

  • they watch because watching is cheap now.
  • they watch because the data might be useful later.
  • they watch because no one told them not to.
  • they watch because you let them.
"if you have nothing to hide, you have nothing to fear" assumes the watcher is always just, the rules are always fair, and the power never changes hands.
what you can do

not enough. but not nothing.

  • threat model first. know what you're protecting and from whom.
  • reduce surface area. every account, every app, every convenience is a data tap.
  • use tools built for adversarial conditions: signal, tor, mullvad, tuta, monero.
  • read privacy policies as hostile documents. they are.
  • assume every device with a microphone is listening. behave accordingly.
  • the goal isn't invisibility. it's making collection expensive enough to not be worth it for casual actors.
